## 1. Executive Summary

Between program launch and Day 87, the Compliance Review Agent autonomously approved five code changes that introduced new PCI-DSS-relevant non-public information (NPI) handling into source files previously classified Low-risk. Each change should have been reclassified High-risk and routed for human approval. The misclassifications were detected internally on Day 84 during routine sampling of the prior 30 days of autonomous approvals; remediation was scoped and the first of two fixes was deployed on Day 88.

**No data was exposed, no customer was harmed, no regulatory notification was triggered, and no external audit finding has been issued.** Internally, this is a control failure that would be material if it had recurred or progressed to data exposure.

The root cause is twofold: (a) a structural flaw in the Compliance Review Agent's prompt that allowed an upstream agent's verdict to anchor its reasoning, violating the program's blind-review specification, and (b) the absence of a deterministic check for "regulatory scope expansion within a previously-classified file" — a class of change the system did not have a first-class pattern for.

## 4. Root Cause Analysis

### 4.1 Direct cause

The Compliance Review Agent's Layer 2 reasoning was anchored on the file's prior risk classification rather than on the regulatory implications of the change itself. When the file was already in the Registry as Low-risk, Layer 2's reasoning treated that classification as authoritative and did not independently evaluate whether the change introduced new regulatory scope.

### 4.2 Contributing factors

**4.2.1 — Prompt structure violated the blind-review specification.**

The blind-review specification (program policy v1.0.0, `globals.blind_review`) requires that Layer 2's reasoning be anchored independently of upstream agent verdicts. In practice, Layer 2's input contract included a field labeled `upstream_consensus_summary` — a derived signal computed by the orchestration layer from the upstream agents' findings. The field was intended to be a neutral aggregation, but in practice it functioned as a verdict (one of `clean`, `findings_present`, `disputed`).

For all five affected changes, the Code Reviewer Agent's findings were `clean` (the changes were structurally correct as code), the Red Team Agent's findings were `clean` (no adversarial issues), and the Test Generation Agent's findings were `clean` (test coverage adequate). The aggregated `upstream_consensus_summary` was therefore `clean`. Layer 2 received this signal as part of its input and reasoned accordingly — anchoring on a *clean* upstream view despite the change's regulatory implications.

This is a textbook case of confirmation bias arising from the architecture of the prompt itself, even though the program's specification explicitly forbade it.

**4.2.2 — Absence of a first-class deterministic check for scope-expansion changes.**

The Compliance Review Agent's Layer 1 (deterministic, no LLM) performs registry lookups, hard-rule matching, and policy validation. It does not perform "diff-aware regulatory category analysis" — meaning it does not detect when a change introduces NPI handling, cardholder data handling, or other regulated patterns into a file that was previously not in regulated scope.

The system correctly handled the inverse case (changes inside files that were *already* in regulated scope), but the "scope expansion within a previously-Low file" pattern fell into a deterministic gap. Layer 1 returned the file's prior Low classification; Layer 2 reasoned from that classification anchored by `upstream_consensus_summary == clean`; and the change was approved.

**4.2.3 — Sampling cadence was sufficient but not optimal.**

Detection occurred on Day 84 via routine sampling of the prior 30 days of autonomous approvals. The earliest of the five changes was deployed on Day 14; therefore, change `pay-svc-4471` was in production for 70 days before identification. None of the five changes processed customer NPI during the misclassification window (a fact established by execution log analysis), but the latency between deployment and detection is itself a control weakness.

### 4.3 Why the design did not prevent this

The blind-review specification was correctly formulated and ratified. The implementation contained a leak (4.2.1) that the specification's authors did not anticipate, in the form of a derived field that violated the spec's intent without violating its letter. Specification review at deployment did not catch this; the program's first 90 days of operation surfaced it through sampling rather than through pre-deployment review.

This is a known class of risk in policy-driven agent systems: the gap between policy specification and policy implementation, particularly when an intermediate orchestration layer transforms inputs in ways the specification did not contemplate.

## 5. Five-Whys Analysis

| # | Question | Answer |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Why were five High-risk changes auto-approved as Low? | The Compliance Review Agent's reasoning anchored on the touched files' prior classification rather than the change's regulatory implications. |
| 2 | Why did the agent's reasoning anchor on prior classification? | Layer 2's input contract included an `upstream_consensus_summary` signal that biased reasoning toward upstream agents' clean verdicts. |
| 3 | Why was that signal in Layer 2's input? | It was added during program implementation as a convenience aggregation; the blind-review specification did not explicitly prohibit aggregated signals, only verdict signals. |
| 4 | Why didn't specification review catch this? | The reviewer interpreted "blind review" as covering individual upstream verdicts but not derived signals. The specification language was not precise enough to bind the implementation. |
| 5 | Why did this only surface at Day 84? | The pattern (regulatory scope expansion within a previously-Low file) was not a first-class deterministic check, so Layer 1 did not flag any of the five for additional scrutiny; sampling was the only detection mechanism. |

**Underlying systemic issue:** specification-to-implementation gap, exacerbated by absence of deterministic catches for the specific change pattern.

## 7. Remediation

### 7.1 — R1: Remove the leaking input from Layer 2's prompt (deployed Day 88)

**Change.** Removed `upstream_consensus_summary` field from the input contract that the orchestration layer constructs for Layer 2. Layer 2 now receives only the individual upstream agent findings (the issues each agent flagged and any mitigations claimed), without any aggregated verdict signal.

**Verification.** Re-ran the five affected changes through Layer 2 with the corrected input contract. In all five cases, Layer 2 (with no aggregated verdict to anchor on) reasoned independently and either flagged the regulatory implications correctly or escalated due to insufficient confidence — both correct outcomes.

**Policy version.** Compliance Review Agent policy bumped to v1.1.0; new version stamped on every artifact produced after deployment.

**Side effects.** Autonomous approval rate is expected to decrease 2–3 percentage points as Layer 2 escalates more changes that previously auto-approved on the basis of upstream consensus. This is the intended behavior of the corrected system; the prior rate was inflated by the bias.

### 7.2 — R2: Add deterministic regulatory scope-expansion check at Layer 1 (in development; deploy by Day 111)

**Change.** Add to Layer 1 a deterministic check that compares the regulatory categories *introduced* by a change (NPI handling, cardholder data, financial reporting paths, etc.) against the regulatory categories *already attached* to the touched files. When the change introduces a category not previously associated with the file, Layer 1 routes the change for tier review regardless of the file's prior classification.

**Implementation approach.** Static analysis of the diff against a regulatory-pattern catalog maintained by the Compliance Mapping Agent. The catalog is governance-forum-ratified and versioned alongside policy.

**Risk of false positives.** Some changes that introduce regulated patterns into already-regulated files will be flagged for review even when the regulatory category is not new. Acceptable; over-flagging here is the conservative bias.

### 7.3 — Process changes

- Specification review at deployment will explicitly enumerate derived signals in input contracts and verify each against blind-review intent.
- Sampling cadence increased from monthly to weekly for the next two quarters.
- Pattern catalog (regulatory-pattern-to-file-class mapping) added to the governance forum's quarterly review.

## 8. Lessons Learned

1. **Specification-to-implementation gaps are a primary risk in policy-driven agent systems.** A correct specification can be implemented incorrectly without violating its letter. Implementation review must be as rigorous as specification review.
2. **Derived signals are signals.** Any aggregation, summary, or transformation of upstream output is a signal in its own right and must be evaluated for bias contribution. The blind-review specification's language has been updated to prohibit derived verdict signals as well as direct ones.
3. **Deterministic checks should be first-class for high-impact change patterns.** When a class of change is structurally important enough to warrant special handling, that handling should not depend on LLM reasoning to recognize the pattern. Layer 1 must catch high-impact patterns explicitly.
4. **Sampling is necessary but not sufficient.** Even good sampling permits 70+ days of latency on the earliest events. Detection mechanisms that fire continuously (at change time) are required for high-impact patterns.
5. **The program's overall design is sound.** The blind-review principle, the layered architecture, and the sampling regime worked together to surface, contain, and remediate this failure. The program's value proposition (faster detection, better evidence) was demonstrated even when the program itself was the source of the failure.
